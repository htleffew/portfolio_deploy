An ADOS-2-aligned screening pipeline, with the uncertainty kept in.

A walk through the architecture I'd build if I were designing a machine-learning assistant for autism screening, three behavioral domains, several model families running in parallel, isotonic calibration, ensemble variance as a confidence band, and a recommendation layer whose tone changes when the models disagree. Built and stress-tested entirely on synthetic data designed to preserve the clinical ambiguity real assessments contain.

Cohort 400 simulated participants mapped to the ADOS-2 rubric, with engineered overlap, noise, and missingness
Architecture Per-domain submodels with soft-voting across five families, isotonic calibration on a held-out fold, and ensemble standard deviation reported as a confidence band
Output A five-tier risk stratification crossed with a four-level confidence axis, written so the recommendation language softens the moment the models begin to disagree
01 / Setup

A research prototype, not a product.

What follows is a design walkthrough for a hypothetical screening assistant aligned with the ADOS-2ADOS-2, Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, Second Edition (Lord, Rutter, DiLavore, Risi, Gotham & Bishop, 2012). The clinician-administered observation rubric used in pediatric autism assessment. Items in the social, communication, and restricted/repetitive domains are coded from semi-structured play-based tasks and combined into a calibrated severity score. rubric. Every number on this page comes from a synthetic cohort of 400 participants I generated to stress-test the pipeline1Synthetic here is the operative word. The features were sampled from distributions designed to mimic the published shape of MediaPipe pose/gaze and Whisper-style speech features in ADOS-2-style sessions. No real recordings, no real participants.. There is no clinical claim and no deployment. The point is to think publicly about what an assistive ML system for this setting would have to commit to in order to be honest about what it does and doesn't know.

The synthesis is deliberately uncomfortable. Across the four profile types I generated, high-functioning ASD, classic ASD, false-positive-risk typical, and typical, features overlap by 60 to 80 percent on every domain. Per-participant Gaussian noise (sigma ~ 25-35% of feature scale) is added to every behavioral count. Ten percent of the cohort has a simulated technical failure on at least one feature, a tracking dropout, an audio gap, an out-of-frame moment, producing genuinely missing values rather than imputed zeros. This is the regime I think any real clinical pipeline will live in, and I wanted to know whether modeling decisions held up there.

None of the architectural moves below are novel on their own. Isotonic calibration is from 2002; tree ensembles for tabular health data are well-trodden; surfacing model disagreement as a clinical signal is a standard recommendation in the digital-phenotyping literature. The contribution, if any, is in the discipline of doing all of them together, in public, and writing down the tradeoffs.

A note on what the synthetic data is for
Synthetic data is the wrong tool for performance claims and the right tool for studying how a pipeline behaves under controlled ambiguity. Because I generated the cohort, I know exactly how much overlap, noise, and missingness each model is being asked to handle, and I can tell when a model's reported AUC is suspiciously high. There is a hard guardrail in the notebook: any model reporting AUC > 0.95 on this cohort is flagged as overfitting the synthesis, not as good, the underlying signal isn't that separable, by construction.
02 / Behavioral features

What gets measured.

The synthetic feature set mirrors what a real pipeline running MediaPipeMediaPipe, Google's open-source library for on-device pose, face mesh, and gaze estimation. Used in published ADOS-2 automation work to derive frame-level gaze direction, joint attention, and motor-mannerism features from session video. (Lugaresi et al., 2019.) face/gaze tracking and WhisperWhisper, OpenAI's open-weights speech recognition model. In ADOS-2 automation work it has been used to time-stamp child vocalizations, detect verbal requests, and segment caregiver speech. F1 on verbal request detection has been reported around 0.94 in clinical pilots.-style speech features over an ADOS-2 session would produce. Each participant is summarized by a vector of session-level statistics, organized into three behavioral domains that match the rubric structure rather than the sensor structure. Domain alignment is the architectural choice that pays for itself later, in calibration, in disagreement detection, and in how the recommendation text is written.

  • Social affect. Gaze-to-face percentage, mutual-gaze episode count, response-to-name latency, social-smile frequency, joint-attention bid count. Maps to ADOS-2 items A1, A2, A7, B1.
  • Communication. Vocalization rate, phrase-repetition rate, conventional-gesture frequency. Maps to B2, C1, D1.
  • Restricted & repetitive behavior. Hand-mannerism duration, close-visual-inspection time, functional-play percentage. Maps to D4 and feeds the RRB-domain submodel.

Within each domain the synthesized values overlap heavily across diagnostic groups. A child with high-functioning ASD and a typically-developing child with social anxiety can produce nearly identical gaze-to-face percentages; the separation, when it exists, lives in joint distributions across domains, not in any single feature. Keeping the domain split through to the modeling stage preserves that.

03 / The overlap problem

No single feature separates the groups.

The first thing the synthetic cohort revealed, exactly as designed, is that no individual behavioral feature separates ASD from non-ASD profiles cleanly. Gaze-to-face percentage, often described in the popular press as the signature of autism, produces overlapping distributions across the four profile types, with the high-functioning ASD group sitting almost entirely inside the typical-development envelope. That is the right outcome. Real-world studies of computer-vision gaze features show the same pattern, and any pipeline that doesn't reproduce it on synthetic data is generating something simpler than the clinical reality2Perochon et al. (2023, Nature Medicine) report AUC ~ 0.90 for autism screening from a multi-feature digital phenotype, but emphasize that any single feature, including gaze, shows substantial group overlap. Separation is achieved through the joint distribution..

Fig. 1 , Synthetic distributions of gaze-to-face percentage by profile type. The high-functioning ASD curve sits almost entirely inside the typical-development envelope; classic ASD shifts left but still overlaps the false-positive-risk typical group. Univariate separation is poor by construction.

This is the entire reason the architecture downstream is plural rather than singular. If gaze alone separated the groups, a logistic regression with one feature would be the right answer and the rest of this writeup would be unnecessary. Because gaze doesn't, communication doesn't, and RRB doesn't, individually, the modeling problem becomes: how do you combine them, how do you handle the missing data, and how do you say something honest when the cross-feature pattern is genuinely ambiguous? Those three questions are what the next several sections answer.

04 / Ensemble

Several models per domain, on purpose.

Each domain has its own submodel, and each submodel is itself a soft-vote ensemble of five model families: logistic regressionLogistic regression, a generalized linear classifier with a sigmoid output. Useful here as a low-variance, high-bias baseline; if its calibrated probability matches the more flexible models, the signal is mostly linear in the feature set., random forest, gradient-boosted trees, XGBoostXGBoost, a gradient-boosted tree library. Handles heterogeneous tabular features without normalization, and per-prediction feature attributions are tractable., and CatBoost. They are not redundant. They have different inductive biases, a linear classifier and a deep tree disagree about the same data in informative ways, and the variance of their predictions is the cleanest signal of epistemic uncertainty I have without a Bayesian neural net.

Aggregation is two-step. Within a domain, the five families are averaged with soft voting (mean of class probabilities). Across the three domains, the calibrated submodel outputs are averaged with equal weights to produce the final risk score. There is no neural meta-learner stitching the streams together, just a weighted mean. I considered learning the cross-domain weights and decided not to: with 400 synthetic participants the meta-learner would mostly be memorizing the synthesis, and the equal-weight prior is more honest about how little the data can tell us.

Fig. 2 , One synthetic participant. Within the communication domain the five model families disagree (range 0.30 -> 0.88, ensemble sigma = 0.19); across domains, social and RRB fire while communication is uncertain. Both are surfaced.
Two flavors of disagreement, both reported
Within-domain variance (sigma across the five model families inside one domain) measures epistemic uncertainty, "the data is consistent with multiple models." Cross-domain spread (max minus min across the three calibrated submodels) measures clinical ambiguity, "the behaviors point in different directions." These are different things, they fire on different cases, and the report distinguishes them. Most pipelines collapse them into a single confidence number; the cost of doing so is that the clinician can't tell whether the model is unsure about the math or unsure about the child.
05 / Calibration

A 0.74 that means 0.74.

A common failure mode in clinical ML is to report a probability that is statistically meaningless. The model says 0.85; the empirical frequency of positives in the 0.85 bin is 0.62. The number is internally consistent but externally a lie. The fix here is per-domain calibration: each submodel's raw ensemble score is passed through a PlattPlatt scaling, fits a logistic regression to the model's raw scores against held-out labels. Smooth, parametric, two parameters. Works well when the score distribution is approximately sigmoid. or isotonicIsotonic regression, fits a non-parametric monotone step function. More flexible than Platt, but needs more data to avoid overfitting the calibration curve itself. regression fit on a held-out split, with the method chosen per domain by cross-validated Brier score. Tree-heavy ensembles tend to prefer isotonic on this synthesis; the linear baseline prefers Platt; the choice is data, not doctrine.

Fig. 3 , Reliability diagram for the social-affect submodel. Raw ensemble scores (red) bow above the diagonal, systematically overconfident in the upper bins. Isotonic-calibrated scores (blue) track the diagonal within +/-2% across all bins.

Calibration doesn't change which cases get flagged, ranking is preserved. What it changes is the meaning of the number a clinician reads. After calibration, "the social-affect submodel says 0.74" means that across many cases scored at 0.74, roughly 74% were eventually labeled positive. That sentence is the entire reason calibration is worth its compute, and it is also the precondition for the within-domain variance from Sec.04 to be interpretable as confidence rather than as numerical noise.

06 / Recommendations

Five risk tiers, four confidence levels, never a diagnosis.

The recommendation layer is built on two orthogonal axes that the clinician sees together but that mean very different things and have to be reasoned about separately, the first being a five-tier risk stratification derived from the calibrated ensemble probability, with cut points at 0.75, 0.60, 0.40, and 0.25 producing the rungs High, Elevated, Moderate, Low-Moderate, and Low, and the second being a four-level confidence axis that reports how the underlying machinery arrived at that probability, ranging from high when at least three of the five model families agreed inside a tight ensemble standard deviation, through moderate and low as agreement weakens, down to single_model for cases the boosted-tree path scored alone because too many features were missing for the linear and forest paths to run honestly.

Fig. 4. The recommendation grid produced by crossing the five-tier calibrated risk stratification (rows) with the four-level confidence axis (columns). Cells in the right two columns are deliberately desaturated and reworded; the strongest urgency language ("priority referral") is reserved for the upper-left region where both the calibrated probability is high and the ensemble agrees, and any case scored from a single model path is flagged in the recommendation text itself.

The crossing of the two axes is what gives the recommendation language its texture, because the same calibrated probability of, say, 0.78 produces a different sentence in the report depending on whether the underlying ensemble agreed or split, with a tight high-confidence ensemble producing the unambiguous priority referral indicated recommendation, a moderately-disagreeing ensemble producing the same tier with an explicit "model families disagreed on this case" note appended, a low-confidence ensemble routing to a softer refer with caution, contributing features were inconsistent across model paths sentence, and a single-model fallback producing language that names the missing-data problem directly so the clinician understands the score is doing less work than it would normally do3The cut points listed in the figure (0.75, 0.60, 0.40, 0.25) are taken straight from the notebook and are placeholders calibrated against the synthetic base rate. On a real cohort they would be re-fit against the local base rate, age-stratified, and re-validated whenever the upstream feature pipeline changes; treating them as fixed for the lifetime of a deployment would be malpractice..

What the system never does, regardless of where a case lands in the grid, is issue a diagnosis. There is no "the model thinks this child has autism" output anywhere in the surface. The strongest thing it will say is priority referral indicated, and even that is paired with the per-domain calibrated scores, the contributing features, and the confidence axis, so the clinician walks into the room knowing not just what the model concluded but how solid the conclusion is. The clinician administers the ADOS-2. The clinician makes the call. The model exists to make the queue shorter and the meeting better-prepared.

07 / Working principles

Six commitments this design makes.

These are the rules I'd want a real clinical pipeline along these lines to have to defend in writing before it touched a patient. None of them are clever; the point is to commit to all six at the same time.

  • Domains are kept apart. Per-domain submodels with their own calibrated outputs. Cross-domain disagreement is signal, not noise to average away.
  • Multiple model families inside each domain. Five families per submodel, soft-voted; their variance is reported as an ensemble sigma, not collapsed into the mean.
  • Probabilities are calibrated. Per-domain Platt or isotonic, picked by held-out Brier. The number means what it says.
  • Recommendations are tiered, not point estimates. Output is one of three tiers, with high-sigma cases routed to a softer tier, the disagreement escape hatch.
  • The output is an evidence summary. Per-domain scores, contributing features, and ensemble disagreement are all surfaced. The clinician reads structure, not a single confident probability.
  • The clinician is the diagnostician. The system never issues a diagnosis. The strongest tier is "priority referral," and even that is paired with the evidence the recommendation rests on.

Committing to all six is what would make a clinical AI artifact along these lines defensible. Drop any one and the rest start to compensate for it: skip calibration and the tiers stop meaning what they say; skip the sigma and the tiers become a smoother UI for raw overconfidence; skip the domain split and the per-feature attribution becomes meaningless to a clinician reading the report. The architecture is the commitments; the commitments are the architecture.

A note on what's missing
Two things this writeup does not address. First, fairness across demographic subgroups: the synthetic cohort wasn't stratified by reported gender or race, and validating that the pipeline doesn't differentially mis-tier subgroups is the next piece of work I would want to do before any of this saw a real participant. Second, drift: the upstream MediaPipe and Whisper-style features change with every model update, and a static calibration is only as good as the version of the feature extractor it was fit against. A real deployment would need a re-calibration cadence and a regression test suite tied to the upstream releases. Until both are done, "this system is calibrated" is true only for the cohort it was fit on, not for any one child showing up at a real clinic next week.
