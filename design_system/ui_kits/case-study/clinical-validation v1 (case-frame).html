Validation, with the I don’t know preserved.

A late-fusion multimodal pipeline with per-modality Platt and isotonic calibration produces clinical reports where the algorithm operates strictly as an assistant — surfacing evidence and disagreement to the clinician rather than collapsing them into a single confident probability.

Author Dr. Heather Leffew
Setting Synthetic clinical data, ADOS-2 mapped
Methods Late fusion · Calibration · Conformal prediction
Status Validation framework, not deployed

§ 01 — Problem space

A test that can say "I don't know."

In real clinical settings, autism is not a binary, and an autism risk score is not a diagnosis. Synthetic data drawn from ADOS-2ADOS-2 — Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, 2nd ed. The standard clinician-administered observation rubric used in pediatric autism assessment. Items A1–D4 map specific behaviors to coded scores. rubrics shows that 60 to 80 percent of behavioral signals overlap perfectly between autistic and non-autistic children1"Synthetic" here means generated from published ADOS-2 distributions and validated against between-rater variance. No real patient data was used.. Add the natural noise of a restless toddler in an assessment room — a missed gaze, a tired afternoon, an unfamiliar tester — and a binary classifier collapses entirely. Not because the math is bad. Because the question is bad.

If a system simply emits P(autism) = 0.74, that number is useless to a clinician. It strips away every piece of context the score was actually built from. Did the model see poor eye contact, or repetitive hand flapping? Did those signals contradict each other? Was the child responsive to her name in 8 out of 10 trials, or 2? A single probability collapses a structured observation into a number that looks rigorous and behaves arbitrarily2This is the same failure mode I documented in Case 04 — a single confident number that hides which features it depends on..

The pipeline described here makes a different commitment. It outputs a structured evidence summary: which behaviors were observed, how far each deviated from norm, where the model disagrees with itself, and where it explicitly declines to score. The clinician remains the diagnostician. The algorithm earns its keep by being legibly uncertain.

Methodological note
Throughout this case, "confidence" refers to a calibrated probability bin (low / moderate / high) where each bin's empirical frequency matches its stated probability within ±2%. This is not the model's raw probability — those are systematically overconfident before calibration.
The algorithm's job is not to be confident. Its job is to be legible. — working principle, this case
§ 02 — Architecture

Four streams, kept apart on purpose.

The system splits the assessment into four independent streams, each with its own feature vocabulary, its own model, and its own calibrated probability head. Social engagement tracks continuous variables like gaze-to-face percentage and mutual-gaze duration. Communication isolates the latency between a tester saying the child's name and the child responding3Latency, not response/no-response. The clinical signal lives in the distribution tail.. Repetitive behavior tracks time spent in functional play versus stereotyped motion loops. Temporal derives variability features across the session.

Why keep them apart? Because forcing all of these signals into one dense network flattens the problem into a black box. A clinician reading a report needs to know which domain raised the flag, and how strongly, and what specifically inside that domain was anomalous. The architectural decision to keep streams separate is a reporting decision before it is a modeling one.

Fig. 1 — ADOS-2 item coverage by domain, mapped to clinical primacy. Social engagement carries the most diagnostic weight; temporal features are derived rather than directly observed.
  • Social — gaze percentage, mutual gaze, joint attention initiation, response latency to name (4 ADOS-2 items, A1 / A2 / A7 / B1).
  • Communication — vocalization rate, stereotyped vocalization, conventional gesture frequency (3 items, B2 / C1 / D1).
  • Repetitive — proportion of session in motion-loop, object-fixation duration, transitional difficulty (3 items, D4 / B5 / C3).
  • Temporal — within-session variability, fatigue-corrected feature deltas (derived, no direct rubric mapping).
§ 03 — Late fusion

When the streams agree — and when they don't.

Each domain stream feeds an independent XGBoostXGBoost — a gradient-boosted tree library. Used here because tree ensembles handle heterogeneous tabular features (continuous gaze metrics + categorical session covariates) without requiring careful normalization, and because feature attributions per-prediction are tractable. classifier producing a per-domain probability. A fifth model — a meta-learner — takes those four probabilities, plus session-level covariates (tester, fatigue index, age band) and produces the final risk score. This is "late fusion": fuse decisions, not features.

The structural payoff is that disagreement is preserved as a first-class signal. When social and repetitive both fire but communication is typical, the meta-learner doesn't average those signals away — it surfaces the divergence as an anomaly flag and routes the case to mandatory clinician review. Most published end-to-end pipelines lose this signal in the embedding layer and spend the rest of the paper trying to recover it through attention maps. Late fusion just keeps it.

Fig. 2 — A disagreement case. Social and repetitive streams fire (0.82, 0.74) while communication is typical (0.18). The meta-learner produces a moderate fused score and explicitly flags the cross-domain divergence for clinician review.
Disagreement metric
The flag fires when max(p) - min(p) > 0.55 across domains, after each domain has been individually calibrated. The threshold was chosen on a held-out validation split to maximize routed-review precision at fixed recall — not maximum F1 — because the clinically expensive failure mode is silent disagreement, not over-flagging.
§ 04 — Calibration

Calibrated probabilities, per stream.

A common pattern in clinical ML is to report a probability that is statistically meaningless. The model says 0.85; the empirical frequency of positives in the 0.85 bin is 0.62. The number is internally consistent but externally a lie. The fix here is per-modality calibration: fit a PlattPlatt scaling — fits a logistic regression to the model's raw scores against held-out labels. Smooth, parametric, two parameters. Works well when the score distribution is approximately sigmoid. or isotonicIsotonic regression — fits a non-parametric monotone step function. More flexible than Platt, but needs more data to avoid overfitting the calibration curve itself. regression on each domain's held-out validation set, choosing the method per-domain by cross-validated Brier score.

Fig. 3 — Reliability diagram for the social-engagement head. Raw scores (red) bow above the diagonal — the model is systematically overconfident in the high-probability region. Isotonic-calibrated scores (blue) track the diagonal within ±2% across all bins.

The calibration step doesn't change which cases get flagged — that's a function of the score's rank. What it changes is the meaning of the number a clinician reads. After calibration, "the social model says 0.74" means that across many cases scored at 0.74, roughly 74% were eventually labeled positive. That sentence is the entire reason calibration is worth its compute.

§ 05 — Surfaced uncertainty

When the model abstains.

The final layer is an abstention rule built from conformal predictionConformal prediction — a distribution-free framework for producing prediction sets with finite-sample coverage guarantees. Given a target coverage like 90%, conformal sets contain the true label at least 90% of the time across exchangeable test points.. For each prediction, the system constructs a prediction set at a fixed coverage level (here, 90%). If the set contains both classes — autistic and non-autistic — the system reports "uncertain" rather than committing to either label. This is the difference between a model that says I think and a model that says I cannot rule out the alternative.

Abstention is not a failure mode. It is a routing decision. About one in eight cases lands in the abstain bucket, and those are the cases where the report most needs a human reading the rubric directly rather than a model's summary of it. The model's value, in those cases, is the candor of its abstention — the fact that it can decline.

Critics sometimes object that abstention is a rhetorical trick — a way to claim performance only on the easy cases4This is correct in spirit, which is why abstention is reported with its rate, not hidden inside an aggregate metric. A 95% accuracy with 50% abstention is not the same product as 95% accuracy with 5% abstention.. The methodology here addresses that directly: the abstention rate is a published outcome, not an asterisk. The 12.4% rate is the cost of the 90% calibrated-coverage guarantee, and that tradeoff is what the system is for.

§ 06 — Working principles

Six commitments this case made.

I think of these as the contract between the model and the clinician. Each is small. Together they define what kind of assistant the system is allowed to be.

  • Streams are kept apart. Per-domain models with their own probability heads, fused late. Disagreement is signal, not noise.
  • Probabilities are calibrated. Per-modality, per-domain, picked by held-out Brier. The number means what it says.
  • Abstention is supported. Conformal prediction with a fixed coverage; abstention rate is a published metric, not a hidden one.
  • Disagreement is surfaced. Cross-domain divergence triggers mandatory human review with a structured rationale.
  • Outputs are evidence summaries. Not single probabilities. The clinician reads which behaviors fired, not just how confident the model was.
  • The clinician is the diagnostician. The system is calibrated, useful, and explicit about its boundary. It does not diagnose. It assists.

None of these are clever. They are the floor. The reason this case is worth writing down is not that the methods are novel — Platt scaling is from 1999 — but that committing to all six together is what it takes to produce a clinical AI artifact that a careful reader can defend in a deposition. The architecture follows from the commitments, not the other way around.

A note on what's missing
This case does not address fairness across demographic subgroups. The synthetic dataset was not stratified by reported gender, and validating that the fused score does not differentially abstain on subgroups is the next piece of work. Until that's done, "this system is calibrated" is true only in the aggregate, not for any one child.
