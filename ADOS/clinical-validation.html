Clinical AI / Bayesian Probability

Uncertainty Quantification in Clinical ADOS.

Why standard binary classifiers fail in environments with severe feature overlap, and how late-fusion Bayesian ensembles mathematically preserve doubt in behavioral diagnostics.

01 / Ambiguity

The Overlap Problem

When technical teams look at behavioral telemetry through a purely statistical lens, they inevitably try to draw sharp lines to separate clinical profiles. But as a clinical psychologist, I had to demonstrate that human psychology doesn't work that way. Gaze-to-face percentage overlaps by nearly 78% between symptomatic and non-symptomatic groups. I routinely observe that a subject with high-functioning ASD and a typically-developing subject with social anxiety can produce nearly identical visual engagement metrics (Lord et al., 2000).

Any pipeline that claims an AUC of > 0.95 on single behavioral features is likely overfitting the distribution rather than finding genuine clinical signal. The underlying human data is simply not that separable.

If we simply dump this tabular array into a single Random Forest or XGBoost model, it will draw artificial decision boundaries through highly ambiguous terrain (Vapnik, 1995). It will force a classification, frequently reporting high artificial confidence on mixed, contradictory human signals. This is the danger of letting data scientists chase a high AUC without clinical context: the model learns to confidently guess rather than admit confusion.

I had to work with the ML engineers to build an architecture that mathematically learned how to hesitate. When human metrics misalign—when a subject exhibits excellent visual engagement, but severe repetitive behavior constraints—the algorithm cannot quietly average these traits and confidently guess "Low Risk." It must systematically flag the internal variance directly to the clinician.

02 / Segregation

Late Fusion Architecture

To capture this necessary hesitation, the architecture employs a Late Fusion Bayesian Ensemble approach. Rather than dumping all fifty behavioral telemetry markers into a single opaque gradient booster, the features are strictly segregated into domain-specific sub-models.

Domain Features Tracked Algorithm
Social Affect Gaze %, Mutual Episodes, Joint Attention Random Forest (Max Depth: 6)
Communication Phrase Repetition, Vocalization Rate XGBoost (L1 Regularization)
Restricted Behavior Functional Play %, Hand Mannerisms Logistic Regression (C=0.3)

Each sub-model operates independently, forced to render a localized probability based solely on its designated behavioral sphere. They are deliberately deprived of cross-domain context. It is only at the terminal node—the Meta-Learner—that these probabilities converge. The Meta-Learner calculates the arithmetic mean for the overall risk score, but critically, it computes the Standard Deviation across the sub-model predictions.

This variance becomes the System Clinical Confidence. If the Social model predicts 80% risk, but the Communication model predicts 10% risk, the mean is 45%. A standard network would quietly report "Moderate Risk, 45%". Our pipeline reports "45% Risk ± 0.35 Variance : LOW CONFIDENCE - MODELS DIVERGE." Thus, mathematical doubt is preserved and surfaced directly to the reviewing clinician.

04 / Thresholds

Implementational Implications

Applying this variance threshold systematically across the 400-patient synthetic scale immediately alters how downstream text generation should be handled. When the standard deviation exceeds 0.25 (as demonstrated in the workbench when inputs drastically conflict), the system's final HTML narrative reporting should dynamically shift tone.

Phrases like "The telemetry suggests an elevated 72% likelihood" must be programmatically replaced with "The telemetry presents conflicting vectors; the 72% mean score carries high model variance, suggesting a mixed behavioral presentation that requires manual clinical review."

This is the fundamental constraint of building predictive architecture for human psychology. Engineering a model to achieve 0.89 AUC on isolated cross-validation folds is mathematically straightforward. Engineering a model that knows when to gracefully admit confusion—and how to communicate that confusion directly to a healthcare provider—is the strict requirement for production deployment.
