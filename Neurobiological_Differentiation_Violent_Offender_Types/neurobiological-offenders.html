03 / Cortical vs. Subcortical Violence Vectors

Cortical vs. Subcortical Violence Vectors

Mitigating violence requires understanding that not all offenders are built the same way. General behavioral categories often fail because two people can commit the exact same crime for completely different biological reasons. By looking at clinical neuroscience, we can understand that severe threat actors generally fall into two distinct profiles: Impulsive Offending (ImO) and Instrumental Offending (InO).

Impulsive Offending (ImO) is driven by dysfunctions in the frontal lobe of the brain. Simply put, this group cannot regulate their emotions. When they get upset, their heart rate spikes, and their biology forces an explosive, emotionally intense outburst (the violence is an end in itself). Conversely, Instrumental Offending (InO) is largely psychopathic. These individuals have severe under-activity in the fear centers of the brain (the amygdala). Because they do not feel normal anxiety or fear responses, they are able to enact cold, calculated violence directly as a means to an end, completely immune to emotional friction.
